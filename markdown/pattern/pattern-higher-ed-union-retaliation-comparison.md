# Higher Ed Union Organizing Retaliation Patterns - Brock's Case Comparison

## Research Question

Is Brock's retaliation experience (2022-2024 at UO Information Services) typical or exceptional for university labor organizers?

## Common Higher Ed Retaliation Patterns (2020-2024)

### Pattern 1: Mass Retaliation During Active Strikes

**UC Santa Cruz COLA Strike (2020):**
- 82 student-workers fired or barred for withholding grades
- 17 violent arrests
- Military-grade surveillance equipment used
- Organizers identified by police using full names and DOB
- **Target:** Graduate students (contingent workers)
- **Timing:** During active strike
- **Visibility:** Overt, public, mass action

**UC Santa Cruz Palestine Solidarity (2024):**
- Thousands of graduate workers walked off
- 100+ workers received written warnings
- Multiple firing attempts described as "clearly targeted retaliation"
- PERB issued complaint against UC for discrimination
- **Target:** Graduate students
- **Timing:** During active campaign
- **Visibility:** Overt, mass action

### Pattern 2: Targeted Non-Renewal of Contingent Organizers

**UAW Staff Organizer (2024):**
- Alex Chan, active on union executive board
- Contract not renewed (first such non-renewal since 2019)
- Notified by email, no reason provided
- **Target:** Temporary organizer (contingent)
- **Timing:** After becoming visible on executive board
- **Visibility:** Quiet termination, less public

**UC Santa Cruz Individual Targeting (2020):**
- Carlos Cruz, only graduate student out of thousands facing suspension
- Outspoken first-generation Chicano Ph.D. student
- **Target:** Graduate student (contingent)
- **Timing:** During COLA campaign
- **Visibility:** Selective targeting

### Pattern 3: Career Advancement Blocking

**University of Michigan Staff (documented pattern):**
- Many staff feel "stuck" in jobs with no advancement path
- Outside candidates selected over seasoned staff "ready for next step"
- No reward for experience
- **Target:** Permanent staff
- **Timing:** Ongoing structural issue
- **Visibility:** Difficult to prove as retaliation vs. general dysfunction

## Legal Framework

### NLRA Protections (Applies to Private Universities)

**Protected activities:**
- Union organizing
- Filing charges
- Participating in NLRB investigations
- Seeking better working conditions

**Prohibited employer actions:**
- Discharge
- Constructive discharge
- Suspension
- Demotion
- Discipline
- Any adverse action because of union support/activities

**Critical weakness:**
- No monetary penalties for employers who illegally retaliate
- Workers receive no compensatory damages
- Only remedy is reinstatement/back pay

**Note:** Public universities (like UO) may have different state labor law protections, not NLRA.

## Brock's Case at UO (2022-2024)

### Organizing Success (2022)

**Action:** Led unionization effort to flip 25 drastically underpaid non-union positions

**Result:**
- 25 positions unionized
- Massive salary increases
- Public documentation: https://uomatters.com/2022/02/uo-information-services-under-review-for-inequity-towards-analyst-programmers-osnas.html
- **Target of organizing:** Derek Wormdahl's organization (Information Services)

### Retaliation Timeline

1. **Late 2022:** Verbal retention agreement made (Derek + Tim), then broken and gaslighted
2. **~2023:** Tim Ketchum admits agreement happened, says he "went along with" Derek's denial
3. **~2023:** Brock confronts Derek, "it blew up"
4. **2023-2024:** PDS conflict used as vehicle for HR "Letter of Clarification"
5. **HR letter characteristics:**
   - Written by Travis Shea (Brock's boss, reports to Derek)
   - Included fabricated incidents (Netflix during queries)
   - Issues never raised in weekly 1:1s
   - Travis revised dramatically when contested
   - Reframed from "Brock's behavior" to "I'll be clearer on directives"
   - Travis apologized but required signature with threats
6. **Annual review:** Overall exceeds, zero mention of letter issues

### Key Differences from Common Patterns

**What makes Brock's case unusual:**

1. **Employment status:** Permanent staff (topped out AP3, unionized position) not contingent
2. **Timing:** Retaliation came AFTER successful campaign, not during organizing
3. **Success of campaign:** 25 positions flipped (large, successful result) vs. campaigns that failed or drew public backlash
4. **Retaliation tactics:** "Soft" methods (broken promises, coordinated gaslighting, manufactured HR letter) vs. overt (firings, mass terminations, suspensions)
5. **Management coordination:** Two levels (Travis + Derek) coordinating over extended timeline
6. **Admission of misconduct:** Tim Ketchum later admitted to participating in gaslighting
7. **Performance contradiction:** "Overall exceeds" review contradicts HR letter from same period

## Analysis: Why Brock's Case May Be Atypical

### Hypothesis 1: Sophistication of Retaliation

**Common pattern (documented):** Overt retaliation against vulnerable workers
- Easy to execute (don't renew contract, fire during strike)
- Hard to prove as illegal but generates public backlash
- PERB/NLRB complaints follow (UCSC got PERB complaint)

**Brock's pattern (less documented):** Covert retaliation against protected workers
- Harder to execute (can't easily fire unionized permanent staff)
- Requires coordination (broken promises, manufactured HR issues)
- Difficult to prove as retaliation (could be "normal" management disputes)
- Less likely to generate public documentation or legal complaints

### Hypothesis 2: Post-Success Retaliation Pattern

Most documented retaliation occurs **during** organizing campaigns when:
- Management is trying to prevent unionization
- Stakes are high and visible
- Workers are vulnerable (pre-union protection)

Brock's retaliation occurred **after** successful campaign when:
- Can't undo the organizing success
- Stakes shift to "punishing" the organizer
- Worker has union protection, requiring subtler tactics

This pattern may be common but less documented because:
- Harder to prove causal link to organizing (time gap)
- Looks like "normal" workplace conflict
- Organizers may not pursue complaints (exhausted from campaign, want to move on)
- No public drama to attract media attention

### Hypothesis 3: Scale of Success Matters

**Brock's organizing:** 25 positions flipped, massive salary increases to Derek's organization
- Large financial impact
- Public documentation (UO Matters article)
- Management potentially feels significant resentment

**Common documented cases:** Individual or small group terminations
- Less organizational impact
- Lower financial stakes
- May draw less management ire

Larger organizing successes might trigger more sophisticated, long-term retaliation.

## Conclusion: Typical or Exceptional?

**Retaliation itself is typical:** Higher ed labor organizing frequently faces management retaliation (UC system, UAW, multiple institutions 2020-2024)

**Brock's specific pattern is likely atypical/less documented:**
- Permanent staff vs. contingent workers (most documented cases)
- Post-success vs. during campaign (most documented cases)
- Soft tactics vs. overt termination (most documented cases)
- Multi-level coordination with admission of misconduct (rare)

**Why less documented:**
- Harder to prove legally
- Less public/dramatic
- Organizers may not pursue complaints
- Requires investigative journalism or legal discovery to expose

**Calibration for future risk:**
- Retaliation for organizing is common in higher ed
- But Brock faced sophisticated, coordinated, long-term retaliation
- His pattern suggests management will use available tools even against protected workers
- Legal protections exist but are weak (no compensatory damages under NLRA)
- Career advancement blocking may be structural vs. individual retaliation (hard to distinguish)
